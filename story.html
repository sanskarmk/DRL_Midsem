Chapter 0 — Opening day

Riya wheels her little tea stall to a street corner. She doesn’t know which teas will sell or when the rush comes — she’ll have to learn by doing. Nobody hands her an instruction manual saying “at 9am sell ginger tea.” She only finds out how good a choice was after she makes it, by counting the money in her tin.

That single sentence is reinforcement learning. Riya is the agent. The street — weather, passers-by, the rival stall — is the environment. Each morning she sees a state (it’s raining, the bus stop is crowded, she’s low on milk), takes an action (brew masala chai, set the price), and the world responds with a reward (₹ profit that hour) and a new state.

One full day from setup to packing up is an episode. The exact play-by-play of that day — situation → choice → money, situation → choice → money — is a trajectory. Riya doesn’t care about a single hour; she cares about the day’s return: the total profit still to come, with today’s rupee valued a touch more than next week’s (that “impatience” is the discount factor \(\gamma\)).

🧮
The one formula under all of it
\[ G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma R_{t+2} + \gamma^2 R_{t+3} + \cdots \]
If the next three hours earn ₹2, ₹3, ₹5 and \(\gamma=0.9\): \(G_0 = 2 + 0.9(3) + 0.81(5) = \mathbf{8.75}\). A small \(\gamma\) makes her short-sighted (grab money now); a large \(\gamma\) makes her patient (build for later).

Chapter 1 — Which tea to feature?

Riya’s corner is fixed and her regulars are whoever walks by. Each morning she can feature one special: masala, ginger, lemon, or plain. Crucially, today’s choice doesn’t change who shows up tomorrow — it only changes today’s takings. Four levers, each with an unknown average payoff, no lasting consequences. This is a multi-armed bandit (think four slot-machine arms).

She keeps a running estimate \(Q(\text{tea})\) of each special’s average profit. Now the eternal tension: exploit (sell the one that’s done best so far) or explore (try another in case it’s secretly better)? Do only the first and she may never discover a winner; do only the second and she wastes money testing duds.

Her rule of thumb is ε-greedy: most days pick the current best, but with a small probability \(\varepsilon\) roll a die and try a random tea. Two cleverer habits: she could start by assuming every tea is amazing (optimistic initial values), which nudges her to try them all early; or she could deliberately re-test the teas she’s least sure about (UCB). And when a festival suddenly changes everyone’s taste (a non-stationary world), she stops giving old days equal weight and switches to a constant step-size \(\alpha\) so recent sales count more.

🧮
How she updates a tea’s estimate
\[ Q \leftarrow Q + \alpha\,[\,\text{today's profit} - Q\,] \]
“Nudge my estimate toward what just happened.” \(\alpha=1/n\) averages everything equally (stable world); a fixed \(\alpha\) (say 0.5) tracks a changing world.
🔖

Chapter 2 — The mobile cart

Business grows, so Riya buys a cart she can move. Everything changes. If she parks near the college today, students become regulars and come back tomorrow — so where she is now shapes where she’ll be next. If she over-sells a gimmicky tea, her reputation dips and future sales fall. Her actions now have consequences that ripple forward.

This is no longer a bandit; it’s a Markov Decision Process. The state genuinely matters now (location, reputation, stock), and choosing an action moves her to a new state according to the world’s dynamics. The Markov part is a comforting simplification: tomorrow depends only on today’s situation, not her entire life story — so she only has to reason about the present state.

To choose well she needs to value the future, not just the next hour. The state value \(V(s)\) is “how much total profit can I expect if I’m in situation \(s\) and keep running things my way?” The action value \(Q(s,a)\) is the same but committing to one action first. These tie together through the Bellman equation: the value of now = the reward I get + the discounted value of wherever I land next.

🧮
Value = reward now + discounted value next
\[ V_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\big[\,G_t \mid S_t=s\,\big], \qquad Q_\pi(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\big[\,G_t \mid S_t=s, A_t=a\,\big] \]
A tempting high-profit tea (big reward) that wrecks her reputation (low next-state value) can have a lower value than a modest, loyalty-building choice. Reward is the hour; value is the whole future.
🔖
Maps to → Topic ③

Chapter 3 — The magic spreadsheet

Imagine the city office gives Riya a perfect spreadsheet: for every spot and every choice, exactly how many customers arrive, how much she’ll earn, and where the crowd drifts next. She now owns a complete model of her world. She doesn’t need to stand in the rain experimenting — she can plan the optimal week at her kitchen table. This is dynamic programming.

She works it in two repeating moves. First policy evaluation: take her current plan and, using the spreadsheet, calculate what each spot is really worth under that plan. Then policy improvement: at each spot, switch to whichever action the numbers now say is best. Evaluate, improve, evaluate, improve… this loop is policy iteration, and it provably climbs to the best possible plan. A shortcut, value iteration, skips the full evaluation and just keeps taking the single best next step until the values settle.

💡
The catch
DP is powerful but needs two luxuries: a complete, accurate model, and a world small enough to sweep every state. Riya rarely has either in real life — which is exactly why the next chapter exists.
🔖
Maps to → Topic ④

Chapter 4 — Learning by living it  Mid-sem core

There is no magic spreadsheet. Riya doesn’t know the city’s dynamics — so she does the only honest thing: she runs full days and writes down what actually happened. This is Monte Carlo learning: no model, no theory, just lived experience averaged over many complete episodes.

At the end of each day she replays it. For every situation she was in, she notes the total profit that followed it (the return), and she keeps a running average across all her days. After enough days, that average is the value of the situation.

🧮
Monte Carlo value = average of returns
If “rainy morning at the college gate” yielded total day-returns of 10, 8 and 12 over three days, then \(V(\text{that state}) = \frac{10+8+12}{3} = \mathbf{10}\). For a specific action there, average the returns that followed that action: returns 5, 7, 6 give \(Q = \mathbf{6}\).

One subtlety: within a single day she might pass through “college gate, rainy” twice. Does she count both? First-visit MC counts only the first time per day; every-visit MC counts every time. First-visit gives cleaner, independent samples; every-visit squeezes more data out of each day. Both converge to the truth.

But averaging only teaches her about choices she actually makes. If she never tries lemon tea on a sunny day, she’ll never learn it’s a hit. So she must keep exploring: either deliberately begin some days in unusual situations (exploring starts), or keep a sliver of randomness in her plan forever (an ε-soft policy, e.g. ε-greedy). Then she runs the full loop — estimate \(Q\) from real days, then shift her plan toward the actions that earned most. That loop is Monte Carlo control. Because she learns from days played under her own (slightly random) plan, this is on-policy learning.

🧮
Keeping the door open — ε-greedy
With \(\varepsilon=0.2\) and 4 choices, her best action still gets played \(1-\varepsilon+\frac{\varepsilon}{4}=0.85\) of the time, and each of the other three gets \(\frac{0.2}{4}=0.05\). Enough randomness to keep learning, enough focus to keep earning.
🔖

Chapter 5 — Not waiting till closing time

Monte Carlo makes Riya wait until she packs up to learn anything. A faster friend whispers: “Why wait? The moment the next hour goes better than you feared, update your gut feeling right then.” That’s temporal-difference (TD) learning — adjust each estimate using the very next reward plus your current guess of what comes after, instead of waiting for the full day’s total.

🧮
TD in one line
\[ V(S_t) \leftarrow V(S_t) + \alpha\big[\,R_{t+1} + \gamma V(S_{t+1}) - V(S_t)\,\big] \]
The bracket is her “surprise.” Note \(\alpha\) is how fast she learns, not how much she explores — a classic exam trap.
🔖
Maps to → Topic ①/⑤ (TD rule)
TD(0) update · TD target · TD error · α as learning rate (not exploration).

Chapter 6 — What's actually on your mid-sem

Riya’s whole story is fair game — except one chapter she hasn’t reached. One day a friend offers to share her sales diary, run with a totally different style, and asks Riya to learn the best plan from someone else’s behaviour. Doing that correctly needs reweighting every day by how likely Riya’s own plan was to act the same way — off-policy learning with importance sampling.

⛔
Off-policy & importance sampling — NOT in your mid-sem
Your professor stated the mid-sem covers Monte Carlo only up to on-policy methods. Off-policy MC, importance sampling, behaviour vs target policy come later — learn the concept (learning from a different policy’s data) but you won’t be tested on the maths.
✅
So your exam playground is Chapters 0–4 (+ the TD rule)
RL vocabulary & returns · bandits & ε-greedy · MDPs & value functions · dynamic programming · and especially Monte Carlo prediction & on-policy control. The guaranteed numericals: compute a return, average returns into \(V\)/\(Q\), first-visit vs every-visit, ε-greedy probabilities, and a small MC-control step.
