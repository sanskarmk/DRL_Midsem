Markov Decision Processes

1. The five components

A finite MDP is a tuple \(\langle \mathcal S,\mathcal A,p,R,\gamma\rangle\):

SymbolNameMeaning
\(\mathcal S\)StatesAll situations the agent can be in
\(\mathcal A\)ActionsChoices available (possibly state-dependent \(\mathcal A(s)\))
\(p(s',r\mid s,a)\)DynamicsProbability of next state \(s'\) and reward \(r\)
\(R\)RewardScalar feedback defining the goal
\(\gamma\)DiscountWeight on future reward, \([0,1]\)

2. The Markov property

💡
“The future depends on the present, not the past”
A state is Markov if it summarises everything relevant: \(\Pr\{S_{t+1},R_{t+1}\mid S_t,A_t\} = \Pr\{S_{t+1},R_{t+1}\mid S_0,A_0,\dots,S_t,A_t\}\). Given the current state and action, the history adds no information. This is what lets us write recursive (Bellman) equations.

The dynamics fully specify the environment. Marginals you’ll use:

\[ p(s'\mid s,a)=\sum_r p(s',r\mid s,a), \qquad r(s,a)=\sum_{s',r} r\,p(s',r\mid s,a) \]

3. Policies and value functions

A policy \(\pi(a\mid s)\) gives the probability of taking action \(a\) in state \(s\). The two value functions measure long-run desirability under \(\pi\):

\[ v_\pi(s)=\mathbb E_\pi\!\big[G_t\mid S_t=s\big], \qquad q_\pi(s,a)=\mathbb E_\pi\!\big[G_t\mid S_t=s,A_t=a\big] \]

\(v_\pi(s)\) = expected return starting from \(s\) and following \(\pi\). \(q_\pi(s,a)\) = expected return starting from \(s\), taking \(a\) first, then following \(\pi\). They’re linked:

\[ v_\pi(s)=\sum_a \pi(a\mid s)\,q_\pi(s,a) \]

4. Bellman expectation equations

Substitute the recursion \(G_t=R_{t+1}+\gamma G_{t+1}\) into the definitions and you get equations that relate a value to its successors:

📐
State-value
\[ v_\pi(s)=\sum_a \pi(a\mid s)\sum_{s',r} p(s',r\mid s,a)\big[r+\gamma\,v_\pi(s')\big] \]
📐
Action-value (the recursive \(q_\pi\) — Jul 2024-makeup Q4b)
\[ q_\pi(s,a)=\sum_{s',r} p(s',r\mid s,a)\Big[r+\gamma\sum_{a'}\pi(a'\mid s')\,q_\pi(s',a')\Big] \]
Its recursive nature means a value is defined in terms of the same function at successor states — this self-consistency is what iterative solvers (DP) exploit.

Worked — recycling-robot q-values (Jul 2024-regular Q4a)

🤖
States {high, low}; from high, search keeps you high w.p. \(\alpha\) (reward \(r_\text{search}\)) or drops you to low w.p. \(1-\alpha\) (also \(r_\text{search}\)). Then:
\[ q_\pi(\text{high},\text{search}) = r_\text{search} + \gamma\big[\alpha\,v_\pi(\text{high}) + (1-\alpha)\,v_\pi(\text{low})\big] \]
And the optimal version replaces \(v_\pi\) with \(v_*=\max_{a'} q_*(\cdot,a')\):
\[ q_*(\text{high},\text{search}) = r_\text{search} + \gamma\big[\alpha\,v_*(\text{high}) + (1-\alpha)\,v_*(\text{low})\big] \]

5. Backup diagrams

A backup diagram shows how value flows from successors back to a state. Open circles = states, solid dots = actions.

Read it top-down: average over actions (weighted by \(\pi\)), then over outcomes (weighted by \(p\)), accumulating reward \(r\) plus discounted successor value.

6. Optimality

The optimal value functions take the max over actions instead of averaging under a fixed policy:

\[ v_*(s)=\max_a \sum_{s',r}p(s',r\mid s,a)\big[r+\gamma v_*(s')\big] \]
\[ q_*(s,a)=\sum_{s',r}p(s',r\mid s,a)\big[r+\gamma \max_{a'} q_*(s',a')\big] \]

An optimal policy is greedy w.r.t. \(q_*\): \(\pi_*(s)=\arg\max_a q_*(s,a)\). Knowing \(q_*\) makes choosing actions trivial — no model or look-ahead needed.

🧭
What you need to find \(v_*\) (Jul 2024-reg Q3c)
To compute the optimal value function you need a complete model: the states, actions, the dynamics \(p(s',r\mid s,a)\) (transition probabilities + rewards), and the discount \(\gamma\). With those, value iteration / policy iteration solves it exactly.

7. Reward scaling and reward signs

✖️
Multiply all rewards by 10? (Jul 2023 Q1d)
Every value scales by 10 too: \(v'(s)=10\,v(s)\) (linearity of the Bellman equation). But the optimal policy is unchanged — multiplying by a positive constant preserves the ordering of action values, and the policy only depends on which action is largest.
➕➖
Do reward signs matter, or only the gaps? (Jul 2024-makeup Q3a)
For episodic tasks where a constant is added to every reward, only the intervals (differences) between rewards matter for the optimal policy — adding a constant \(c\) to all rewards shifts every return by the same amount and doesn’t change which policy is best. But in continuing tasks a constant added to every step changes the infinite discounted sum unequally, so it can matter. The classic gridworld caveat: adding a constant can remove the urgency to reach the goal if it makes “stalling” rewarding.

8. How to formulate any scenario as an MDP

A reliable recipe (use it for Dec 2025 Q2, Jul 2023 Q1, any “formulate the MDP” prompt):

  1. States — what does the agent need to know to decide? (ICU: {lowO₂, OptimalO₂, highO₂}.)
  2. Actions — the controllable choices. (ICU: {increase, decrease, maintain} pressure.)
  3. Transitions \(p(s'\mid s,a)\) — including given probabilities (ICU: increase/decrease leaves health unchanged w.p. 0.4; maintain always preserves state) and equal-likelihood assumptions for the rest.
  4. Rewards — encode the goal (reach OptimalO₂ good; over-ventilation / highO₂ bad).
  5. Discount \(\gamma\) and the terminal condition (four consecutive Optimal readings ⇒ stabilised).
⚠️
Reward-design pitfall (Dec 2025 Q2b)
Rewarding only “lowO₂ → OptimalO₂” (+10) encourages reaching the target but says nothing about leaving it. Penalising “OptimalO₂ → highO₂” (−70) actively discourages over-ventilation. Design B is safer when over-ventilation is the real danger; combine both for a policy that both reaches and stays in the safe zone.

9. Check yourself

Q1. \(q_\pi(s,a)\) differs from \(v_\pi(s)\) because it…
fixes the first action to \(a\), then follows \(\pi\)
ignores the discount factor
is only defined for terminal states
requires no policy
\(q_\pi(s,a)\) commits to action \(a\) now and follows \(\pi\) afterward; \(v_\pi(s)=\sum_a\pi(a|s)q_\pi(s,a)\).
Q2. You multiply every reward in an MDP by 10. The optimal policy…
is unchanged; values scale by 10
changes completely
becomes random
is undefined
Positive scaling preserves the ordering of action-values, so \(\arg\max\) (the policy) is unchanged; only the magnitude of \(v\) scales.
Q3. The Bellman optimality equation differs from the expectation equation by…
dropping the discount
replacing \(\sum_a\pi(a|s)\) with \(\max_a\)
removing the reward term
summing over time instead of states
Optimality takes the best action (max) rather than averaging over a fixed policy.
